Omnidirectional Connectivity and Past Mobility: A Circuit Theory Analysis of Iron Age Bornholm
Situated centrally in the Baltic Sea, the island of Bornholm served as a vital hub for transport, trade, and cultural connection during the Iron Age (c. 100 BC – AD 1000). This geographic advantage resulted in geopolitical instability, spurring the construction of six prominent inland fortifications that utilized the island's rocky topography and natural barriers. In this project, I investigate how these rural fortifications were spatially organized to control regional movement, and how the isolation of these forts evolved during a major 5th-century demographic shift to the coast (Albris et al., 2025).
	Previous spatial analyses of past mobility have relied heavily on deterministic Least Cost Path (LCP) models. These models assume that travelers possessed perfect landscape knowledge and strictly uti-lized a single, optimized route, fundamentally ignoring the uncertainty, exploratory behavior, and chang-ing alternating routes of actual human travel (Howey, 2011). To fill this methodological gap, I apply cir-cuit theory to model movement as a probabilistic flow of "current" across a conductive landscape, which serves as a dynamic and robust alternative for archaeological research (McRae et al., 2008).
To capture how the landscape and human mobility changed over time, I developed a detailed workflow combining Geographic Information Systems (GIS) and Machine Learning:
	To accurately model past mobility using circuit theory, it is first necessary to create a "resistance surface" that reflects the physical cost of travel across the landscape. Adapting the methodology of Howey (2011), I will construct a resistance model using a combination of historical cartography and geomorphological data. Early 19th-century cadastral maps (as used in the FORTIS project) will serve as a pre-modern baseline to identify historical vegetation and land-use zones, such as dense woodlands, bogs, and heathlands. Because historical maps often lack precise topographical details, these features will be draped over high-resolution modern lidar scans and surface geology maps to accurately interpo-late their natural boundaries (Comer et al., 1995). Each identified land-cover category is then assigned a specific friction value and combined with topographic slope data. This approach translates historical maps and physical geomorphology into a unified resistance surface, establishing the necessary founda-tion to run the circuit theory models.
	I also propose an untested methodology for adding additional details to the resistance surface  focusing on paleoenvironmental reconstruction using the Landscape Reconstruction Algorithm (LRA) (Sugita, 2007). By applying the REVEALS and LOVE models to pollen records, the method corrects the non-linear biases of raw pollen percentages to provide quantitative estimates of past plant cover. Be-cause this approach has been extensively validated in southern Scandinavian contexts (Sugita et al., 2008) and scaled across Europe (Githumbi et al., 2022), it ensures highly accurate, phase-specific em-pirical resistance surfaces for the periods before and after the 5th-century settlement shift.
	Omnidirectional circuit theory models are applied across both time periods to map shifting move-ment corridors (Pelletier et al., 2014). To execute this, software packages such as gflow are used (Leon-ard et al., 2017). This allows the omnidirectional analysis to be processed at a highly refined spatial resolution without “coarse-graining” the data, ensuring that fine-scale topographical bottlenecks are accurately preserved in the models. This is done alongside pairwise circuit analyses to calculate chang-es in "effective resistance" between specific archaeological locations.
	Hierarchical Corridor Classification: Beyond basic connectivity, the analysis will evaluate how po-tential mobility impacted the settlement hierarchy. Following the approach of McLean and Rubio-Campillo (2022), the modeled current flow will be quantitatively compared against the archaeological significance of different fortifications and “hubs”. This allows the output to be classified into primary, secondary, and tertiary corridors, mapping which specific routes were most vital to the island's socio-political structure without relying on modern geographic proxies.
	Network Optimization: Finally, I incorporate visibility network analysis to assess if warning beacons and forts functioned as "Steiner points", artificial junctions constructed within an engineered, island-wide communication system (Verhagen, Polla and Frommer, 2014).[MJ1.1]
	My proposed analysis aims to identify high-current "pinch points", natural topographical bottle-necks, and test whether they strongly correlate with the initial placement of the inland fortifications. This will help evaluate if there was a deliberate military strategy to monopolize regional traffic flows ra-ther than simply minimizing travel costs. In military-controlled areas, where you can go depends on more than just physical effort, you also have to deal with possible checkpoints, rules, and danger. By incorporating Spatial Absorbing Markov Chains (SAMC) into the circuit framework, the model will explic-itly disentangle the physical cost of movement from the spatial risk of travel failure or 'mortality' (e.g., the risk of military interception or surveillance) (Fletcher et al., 2019; Kervellec et al., 2024). This allows for an assessment of whether fortifications were placed to maximize surveillance and travel risk for ad-versaries travelling on the island.
	By comparing the two time periods, I plan to quantitatively measure changes in effective resistance to assess the isolation these forts may have experienced as populations gradually relocated to the coast. To separate what the landscape forces people to do from what they actually intend to do, the Pair Distribution Function (PDF) is applied to observed settlement patterns, revealing distance preferences decoupled from geographical limitations. (Boucherie, Maier and Lehmann, 2025).
	To ensure the reliability of these future findings against the fragmentary nature of archaeological data, I will apply a Monte Carlo simulation framework generating thousands of "robustness scenarios" to verify the robustness of the modeled corridors [MJ2.1](Herrera Malatesta and de Valeriola, 2024). Ultimately, this project seeks to demonstrate that circuit theory provides a vastly more realistic and ro-bust framework for understanding ancient mobility than traditional deterministic methods. By disentan-gling geographical limitations from human intentionality, this methodology promises to offer landscape archaeology a powerful new tool for predicting how past societies navigated, engineered, and con-trolled their changing environments.
