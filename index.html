Geopolitical Instability on Iron Age Bornholm

  • Central Baltic Hub: Vital for transport, trade, and cultural connection (c. 100 BC – AD 1000), leading to geopolitical instability.
  • Defensive Adaptation: Construction of six prominent inland fortifications utilizing rocky topography and natural barriers.
  • Project Aim: Investigating how these rural fortifications were spatially organized to control regional movement.
  • Demographic Shift: Assessing how the isolation of these forts evolved during a major 5th-century relocation to the coast (Albris et al., 2025).

The Methodological Gap in Modeling Mobility

Deterministic LCP Models

  • Rely on basic Least Cost Path (LCP) networks.
  • Assume travelers possessed perfect landscape knowledge.
  • Strictly utilize a single, mathematically optimized route.
  • Ignores natural uncertainty, exploratory behavior, and changing alternating routes of actual human travel (Howey, 2011).

Circuit Theory Alternative

  • Models movement as a probabilistic flow of "current" across a conductive landscape.
  • Evaluates traversal across the environment.
  • Accounts for uncertainty and variations in human travel.
  • Provides a dynamic and robust alternative essential for archaeological research (McRae et al., 2008).

Phase I: Resistance Surface Construction

  • Historical Cartography: Historical maps establish a pre-modern baseline for vegetation and land-use.
  • Geomorphological Integration: Historical features draped over high-resolution lidar and surface geology to interpolate precise boundaries (Comer et al., 1995).
  • Unified Friction Model: Land-cover and topographic slope assigned specific friction values to model physical travel cost (Howey, 2011).

Proposed Untested Methodology

Adding empirical details via the Landscape Reconstruction Algorithm (LRA) to correct pollen biases and provide quantitative estimates of past plant cover (Sugita, 2007; Sugita et al., 2008; Githumbi et al., 2022).

Phase II: High-Resolution Probabilistic Flow

  • Mapping shifting movement corridors with omnidirectional circuit theory models (Pelletier et al., 2014).
  • Executed with highly parallelized gflow software (Leonard et al., 2017).
  • Processed at a refined spatial resolution without coarse-graining the data.
  • Ensures fine-scale topographical bottlenecks are accurately preserved.
  • Calculates changes in "effective resistance" between specific settlements.

Corridor Classification

Hierarchical Classification

Current flow is ranked into primary, secondary, and tertiary corridors to show which routes mattered most for movement and control (McLean and Rubio-Campillo, 2022).

  • Primary: main high-flow connections
  • Secondary: supporting routes
  • Tertiary: local low-flow links

Conclusion

Key Message

Circuit theory is applied as a dynamic, probabilistic alternative to deterministic path models, combining resistance-surface reconstruction, omnidirectional and pairwise flow analysis, and robustness testing to investigate mobility, fortification control, and settlement hierarchy on Iron Age Bornholm.

Why It Matters

  • Moves beyond single-route assumptions by modeling multiple plausible pathways and bottlenecks.
  • Supports phase-to-phase comparison of corridor structure and effective resistance during the 5th-century coastal shift.
  • Provides an explicit framework to separate landscape constraints, travel risk, and potential human intent under uncertainty (Herrera Malatesta and de Valeriola, 2024).
